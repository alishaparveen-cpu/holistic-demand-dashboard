Why GMB Is Not the Issue

A walk-through of what we checked, what we found, and where the actual problem is hiding.
Allo · STI tagging investigation · Prepared 2026-05-21 · Window: last 30 days · 56 GMB locations · 4 channels · 48 clinics with ≥10 consultations
If you only read one thing, read this.
For four months, the working theory has been: "Some of our clinics are tagging very few STI patients because their Google listing isn't good enough." We tested that theory four different ways and it doesn't hold. The number of STI-tagged consultations at a clinic is not explained by anything visible in Google — not the profile, not the photos, not the keywords, not the channel mix. It is explained by what happens inside the clinic, after the patient has already arrived. This document walks through how we got to that answer.

0 · What you need to know before reading

This report uses a handful of terms that come up everywhere. If any of them are new, skim the box below — the rest of the report assumes you've read this.

Allo clinics
The 56-clinic network this investigation covers. Each clinic sees patients in person for sexual-health consultations (sexologist, STI, ED, PE, etc.).
GMB (Google My Business)
Also called "Google Business Profile." It's the listing for each clinic that shows up when somebody Googles "sexologist near me" or finds the clinic on Google Maps. Each Allo clinic has its own GMB listing with photos, a description, opening hours, phone number, and so on.
STI
Sexually Transmitted Infection — chlamydia, gonorrhoea, HIV, syphilis, herpes, etc. One of the conditions Allo treats.
STI share / STI%
The percentage of a clinic's completed consultations where the doctor recorded "STI" as the diagnosis category in the EMR. If a clinic had 100 consultations and 30 were tagged STI, its STI share is 30%. This is the metric the whole investigation is about.
Top / Mid / Low tier
We bucketed clinics by their STI share so we could compare them: Top = STI share ≥28% (10 clinics, e.g. Velachery, Mogappair, HSR Layout), Mid = 12–28% (27 clinics), Low = under 12% (10 clinics, e.g. Kalyan West, Chinchwad, Garkheda). The business question: why is Low so much worse than Top?
Channel
How a patient first found the clinic. The four channels in this report are:
  • GMB — patient found the clinic via Google Maps / Google search and clicked the GMB listing.
  • Google (paid) — patient clicked a Google Ads result.
  • Organic / direct — patient came directly to the Allo website, or via an organic (non-ad) link.
  • Practo — patient booked via the Practo marketplace.
UTM source
A small tag stored on the patient's lead record that says which channel they came from. Lets us link every consultation back to where the patient was first acquired.
encounter_tags
The Redshift table where doctors record the diagnosis category for each completed consultation. Each row says (patient, consultation, tag_type). A consultation gets counted as "STI" only if a row exists in this table with tag_type='sti'. If the doctor doesn't write this row, the consultation is invisible to STI share, no matter what was actually treated.
Truth STI%
A separate STI% figure maintained by the QS2 / operations team, against an internal definition. We treat it as the ground truth and compare our Redshift number against it.

1 · The business question — and why this report exists

Allo wants to grow STI consultations. STI patients are a high-LTV segment and Allo's brand position is built on STI care. Some clinics consistently book 30–50% of their consults as STI. Others book 2–10%. The gap is large enough that fixing the low-tier clinics would meaningfully grow the business — if we knew what to fix.

The intuition going in was that GMB was the problem. The thinking went: "If Velachery Chennai is at 39% STI and Kalyan West Mumbai is at 4%, maybe Velachery's GMB listing is better tuned for STI searchers — better description, more photos, the right categories, the right keywords." So we audited GMB four different ways. None of them produced an explanation.

This report walks through each round of the audit, what it ruled out, what the data actually shows, and where we now think the problem lives.

2 · The journey of a patient — and where things can go wrong

Before we dive into the data, it helps to picture the path a patient takes from typing a search into Google to ending up as a row in STI share. Every step is a place where something could leak.

1 · Patient searchesThey Google "sti test", or "sexologist near me", or click a Google Ad, or open Practo.
▼
2 · They reach a clinic's listing / pageGMB profile, ad landing page, Practo profile, Allo website. The listing has to show up and make them click.
▼
3 · They book / walk inPhone call, online appointment, or walk-in. UTM source is stamped on the lead at this point.
▼
4 · The doctor consults themThe doctor diagnoses whatever the patient came in for.
▼
5 · The doctor records a tag in encounter_tagsIf tag_type='sti' is recorded, the consultation counts toward STI share. If not, it doesn't — regardless of what was actually treated.
The "GMB is the problem" theory says the leak is between steps 1 and 2 — patients searching for STI help aren't reaching low-tier clinics' listings, or aren't being convinced to click. Everything that follows in this report tests that, and finds the leak is actually at step 5.

3 · Round 1 — Are the GMB profiles different? (No.)

The hypothesis: top-tier clinics have more polished GMB listings — more photos, better descriptions, the right categories set, a working appointment URL, an LGBTQ+-friendly flag, etc.

What we did: pulled every GMB profile field for all 56 locations (via locations.xlsx + the GBP API), bucketed by tier, and looked at the percentage of clinics in each tier with each feature set.

Chart 1 · Each bar pair shows the % of clinics in that tier that have a given GMB feature filled in. Purple = top, amber = low.

How to read this: every GMB-profile field we checked is filled in at the same rate at top and low tier. 100% of top clinics have a primary category set; 100% of low clinics do too. 100% have a description, 100% have a cover photo, 100% have other photos, 0% have the LGBTQ+ flag (universally missing), 100% have a WhatsApp URL. The profiles are essentially identical.

Ruled out Profile completeness isn't the lever. If you made every low-tier clinic's profile look like a top clinic's, nothing would change — they already look the same.

4 · Round 2 — Do top clinics get more GMB exposure? (Yes, but not enough to explain the gap.)

The hypothesis: maybe profile content is the same, but top clinics' listings are simply seen more — more impressions, more calls, more direction taps. Volume could be the driver.

What we did: pulled the 30-day GMB performance numbers (impressions, phone calls, website clicks, direction taps) from insights.csv, averaged per clinic in each tier, and compared.

Chart 2 · Average per-clinic 30-day numbers. Top clinics get ~2.6× more impressions, but only modestly more phone calls and website clicks, and they actually get fewer direction taps.

How to read this: top-tier clinics do get more impressions on Google (11,091 vs 4,276 — a 2.6× gap). But the STI share gap between top and low is ~6× (35% vs 6%). And the action metrics that matter more — phone calls, website clicks, direction taps — are not 2.6× higher; some are almost identical and direction taps are actually higher at low tier. So even if low-tier impressions were doubled, the math doesn't get you anywhere near the STI gap.

Ruled out (as the primary driver) Exposure helps but cannot explain a 6× gap. Something else is doing most of the work.

5 · Round 3 — Are top clinics getting different search intent? (No.)

The hypothesis: people searching for top clinics use STI-specific terms ("sti test", "hiv test") while people reaching low clinics use generic terms — so top clinics naturally have more STI-shaped demand walking in the door.

What we did: pulled the raw search-keyword data for each GMB profile from the GBP Performance API (5,175 keyword-impression rows across 64 locations). Classified each keyword into one of five buckets:

  • STI/STD intent — "sti", "std", "hiv test", "syphilis treatment", etc.
  • Brand — "allo", "allo health", "allo clinic"
  • Sexologist — "sexologist", "sexologist near me", "sexology clinic"
  • Sex problem — "erectile dysfunction", "ed treatment", etc.
  • Generic — "clinic near me", "doctor", everything else

Then we summed the impressions per bucket per tier.

Chart 3 · How the search demand reaching each tier breaks down by intent. Bars sum to 100% of impressions in that tier.

How to read this: the STI/STD intent share is identical across tiers — 5% at top, 6% at mid, 5% at low. Brand and sexologist queries are nearly identical too. Generic queries are slightly higher at top (67% vs 55%). Whatever differentiates top and low clinics, it is NOT that top clinics are receiving more STI-shaped search demand. They are receiving the same mix. In some buckets low-tier even gets more.

Ruled out Search intent reaching the profile is not the lever.

6 · The smoking gun — high STI demand, low STI tagging

At this point in the investigation we had a paradox. Let's plot each clinic with STI-keyword demand on one axis and STI tagging rate on the other. If GMB were the issue, we'd expect a diagonal — more STI demand → more STI consults. Here's what we actually see.

Chart 4 · Each dot is one clinic. X = % of GMB searches that are STI-intent. Y = % of consultations tagged STI. The relationship has disappeared.
The clearest paired example: Panvel (Mumbai) gets 10.6% STI-intent search demand on its GMB profile and tags 7% of consults as STI. Chinchwad (Pune) gets 11.4% and tags 6%. Meanwhile Velachery (Chennai) gets almost no STI search demand (3.3%) and tags 39% of consultations as STI. The clinics getting more STI-shaped demand are tagging fewer STI cases. If GMB were the lever, this pattern would be impossible.

7 · Round 4 — The channel breakdown (this is the new piece)

By this point three of the most obvious GMB-side explanations were dead. The last remaining acquisition-side explanation was: maybe GMB is genuinely weak, but top clinics make up for it through other channels. Maybe Velachery's STI patients all come via Practo or Google Ads, and GMB is incidentally low — that would mean GMB isn't the issue at Velachery because GMB isn't doing the work there in the first place.

To test that, we joined every completed consultation to its lead's UTM source and computed STI tagging rate per channel per tier.

What we did, step by step

  1. Pulled every completed consultation from the last 30 days (Redshift, allo_analytics schema).
  2. For each consultation, looked up the patient's first lead and read its utm_source: GMB, Google, Organic, or Practo.
  3. Checked encounter_tags for that consultation — does it have a row with tag_type='sti'?
  4. For each clinic × channel combination, computed: STI% = consultations tagged STI ÷ total consultations.
  5. Averaged within each tier.

What we found

How to read this table. Find the GMB column. Top-tier clinics tag 35% of their GMB-sourced patients as STI. Low-tier tag 10%. Now look at Google: top is 26%, low is 7%. Organic: 25% → 8%. Practo: 15% → 3%. Every single channel collapses by roughly the same factor at low-tier clinics. It doesn't matter how the patient got there.

Chart 5 · The same data as the table above, in bars. Notice how the shape of all four channels is the same — high at top, low at low. There is no channel where low-tier clinics tag normally.

Why this is the smoking gun

If GMB were the problem, we'd expect GMB to crater at low-tier clinics while Practo / Google / Organic still converted normally — because those patients didn't come through GMB. They came through completely different acquisition paths.

But Practo patients also get tagged at 2% at low-tier clinics. So do organic walk-ins. So do paid-search patients. The only thing all four channels share is what happens after the patient arrives — the consultation and the tag.

Chart 6 · "How many times worse low-tier clinics convert" computed as Top STI% ÷ Low STI%. If the problem were channel-specific, one bar would tower over the others. They're all in the 3–8× band — a uniform clinic-level collapse. (Practo runs higher mainly because Practo's STI conversion is universally weak even at top tier.)

9 · Two clinics where the data itself is fishy

Two clinics show a gap between the "truth" STI% (from the QS2 operations sheet) and what we measure in Redshift. They're worth calling out because they tell us something about the definition itself.

Chart 8 · Purple = truth STI% (QS2 sheet). Amber = our Redshift STI% (computed from encounter_tags with tag_type='sti'). Notice Garkheda — truth says 7%, Redshift says 0%.
  • Garkheda (Aurangabad). Truth says 7% STI (2 of 29 consults), Redshift says 0% across every channel. There are zero rows with tag_type='sti' for this clinic in the last 30 days. The two STI cases that truth counts must be tagged under a different category — possibly an STI screening or test order recorded as others or as a lab order in allo_drugs / allo_labs.
  • Saraswathipuram (Mysuru). Truth 7%, Redshift 3% (2 of 71). Same direction of gap.

Both suggest the "STI calls" definition in the truth sheet counts something broader than tag_type='sti'. This needs resolving — if the truth definition includes screenings and tests, the whole low-tier ranking could partly be a query artifact. We'd recommend reconciling this before any per-clinic operational action.

10 · So where does the actual problem live?

Pulling it all together — here's the score from the four rounds of audit:

Hypothesis we testedWhat we expected if it were the answerWhat the data showsVerdict
1. GMB profile completeness is worse at low tier Low-tier missing descriptions, photos, categories Identical across tiers (100% vs 100% on every field) Ruled out
2. Low-tier clinics get less GMB exposure Big gap in impressions / calls / clicks / direction taps 2.6× impressions gap, near-zero action-metric gap. Not enough to explain 6× STI gap. Ruled out as primary
3. Low-tier clinics get less STI-intent search demand Lower share of "sti test" / "hiv test" queries Identical 5% STI-keyword share across tiers. Lowest STI% clinic has highest STI demand. Ruled out
4. Top clinics get STI patients from non-GMB channels; GMB isn't doing the work GMB STI% similar across tiers, but Practo / Google / Organic carry top tier Every channel — GMB, Google, Organic, Practo — collapses by 3–8× at low tier Ruled out

All four acquisition-side explanations are dead. The remaining hypothesis — and now the only one consistent with the data — is that the difference between a top-tier and a low-tier clinic is what happens during and after the consultation, not what happens before it.

Working hypothesis. When a patient is consulted at Velachery, the doctor records an STI tag in encounter_tags 45% of the time on GMB-sourced consultations. When the same kind of patient is consulted at Garkheda, the doctor records an STI tag 0% of the time. The variance is so uniform across all four acquisition channels that the explanation has to live at the only stage all four share — the consultation and the tagging.

Three concrete mechanisms that could produce this

  1. Tag substitution. The doctor sees an STI patient and records the tag as ed_plus, pe_plus, or others instead of sti. STI share collapses by definition even though STI is being treated.
  2. Tag omission. The doctor doesn't write any tag at all. The consultation is completed in the EMR but encounter_tags is empty.
  3. Intake-script difference. The receptionist frames walk-ins as a generic "consultation" rather than asking the STI-specific intake question. The doctor then follows the framing on file rather than re-classifying.

All three are testable from the EMR. The next-step plan in §11 distinguishes between them.

11 · Recommended next steps

Step 1 — EMR tagging audit (highest priority). Pull the last 30 days of consultations at Garkheda, Saraswathipuram, Kalyan West, and Chinchwad (the 4 worst by tagging gap). For each completed consultation, answer:
  • Was any row written to encounter_tags?
  • If yes, what tag_type? (sti / ed_plus / pe_plus / others)
  • Was an STI test ordered via allo_drugs or allo_labs?
  • Is one specific doctor responsible for the under-tagging at each clinic?
This single query distinguishes between substitution, omission, and intake.
Step 2 — Controlled cross-clinic patient comparison. Use phone_no or patient_id to find patients who visited a top-tier and a low-tier clinic. Compare their tag histories at each. If the same person gets tagged STI at Velachery and not at Kalyan West, that's the cleanest possible proof that the variance is the tagger, not the patient.
Step 3 — Reconcile truth-vs-Redshift definitions. Pull raw encounter_tags rows for Garkheda and Saraswathipuram. If the QS2 "STI calls" definition counts tag_types outside 'sti' (e.g. screenings, test orders), the whole low-tier ranking needs to be re-cut against the correct definition before we act on it.
Step 4 — If steps 1–3 confirm tagging issues, train low-tier clinic teams on STI tagging. This is a 30-minute training intervention per clinic — not a marketing campaign, not a GMB campaign, not a paid-search rebid.

What this means for the GMB workstream specifically

  • Housekeeping fixes still worth doing (logos on all 56, appointment URLs everywhere, fixing Vijayanagar's 0-impression listing, creating the missing Surat/Bhimrad listing, fixing Tatya Tope Nagar's wrong-city title). Treat these as hygiene, not as STI-share levers. Don't expect a measurable lift on STI%.
  • Don't invest in GMB acquisition to fix STI share. Investing in GMB acquisition while tagging is broken will inflate the gap between truth and Redshift, not close it.
  • If you ever do want to grow STI volume, GMB is still the right channel. It is the strongest STI converter at every tier (35% at top, vs Practo's 15%). But the clinic ops side has to be functioning first.
  • Practo is not a credible STI lever. Practo's STI conversion is universally weak (15% even at the top, 2% at the bottom). Practo's lead pool is structurally non-STI.

12 · Caveats and limits of this analysis

  • Consult counts are about 2× the truth volume. Spot-check May 11–17: Redshift returns 2,932 consults, truth says 1,463. The ratios in this report hold up, but absolute consult numbers are inflated. The "Practo 39, 1 STI" patterns are directionally correct, not literally correct.
  • The window is 30 days. Matches the GMB insights API window so volumes line up. The QS2 truth sheet appears to be on a different window — that's part of why the absolute numbers diverge.
  • Two clinics with truth STI > 0 but Redshift STI = 0 (Garkheda, Saraswathipuram) suggest the truth "STI calls" definition is broader than tag_type='sti' alone. This is the most important caveat — resolving it could move the low-tier ranking. See step 3 in §11.
  • Channel mapping depends on UTM source quality. If a lot of leads have a missing or misattributed UTM, the channel rows in §7 could be noisier than they appear. We did spot-check the distribution and it looked clean, but a full UTM-quality audit would be a useful follow-up.
